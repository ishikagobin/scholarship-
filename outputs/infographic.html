Before birth
The decision to start a family
1960
A given
In 1960, 72% of adults were married. A single earner at the median wage could support a family of four. Having children was the default — the question was how many, not whether.
2024
A calculation
In 2024, only 47% of households include a married couple. 71% of Americans say having children is not affordable for most people. The decision that was once assumed is now subject to spreadsheets.

What the data shows

The child penalty
has more than doubled.

01

The new cost is childcare

In 1960 childcare was not a budget category — it simply did not exist for most families. Today the national median infant center-based care costs $9,025 per year. In high-cost cities it exceeds $25,000. This single new expense explains most of the divergence between what parenthood cost then and what it costs now.

02

Healthcare grew 14× faster than wages

Hospital services rose 14.2× in real terms since 1967, while average wages rose roughly 3× over the same period. A delivery that cost the equivalent of $2,060 in 1960 now costs $18,865 — before any complications, before the anesthesiologist's separate bill.

03

College became a second mortgage

Public university tuition rose 14.9× in real terms since 1978. A year at a public university cost $2,808 in today's dollars in 1970; it costs $11,260 today in tuition alone, before room and board. For a middle-income family planning four years, that is $45,000 in tuition at today's prices versus $11,000 in 1970 dollars.

04

The fertility rate is the verdict

The U.S. total fertility rate hit a record low of 1.60 in 2024 — well below the 2.1 replacement level. Research estimates rising housing costs alone explain approximately 51% of the fertility decline between the 2000s and 2010s (Couillard, 2024). The cost of a child, not the desire for one, is what changed.
