Eleanor
1965  ·  The Baseline

Age 29. Median-income household. Her husband works full time so she stays home with their two children. The family owns their home at 3.4× their annual income.

Tiffany
Today  ·  Median America

Age 29. Works full time at a median income of around $57,000. She rents — a home costs more than 4.5× her annual income. Infant childcare alone runs $9,025 a year: 21% of what she earns. She wants a home and two children. The math keeps not working out.

Destiny
Today  ·  Top 20%

Age 34. Earns $113,000 — top quintile. She owns her home and is building equity. The same $9,025 in childcare costs Tiffany 21% of her income; it costs Destiny 8%. Same costs. Different math. The dream is still possible — but only from where she is standing.

1953–1973

The dream once worked

The American Dream was never equally available to everyone. But for a broad middle class, income growth, housing affordability, and family formation were more aligned than at any point since.

The Shared Boom Was Real - Income nearly doubled in a generation then it slowed down
Between 1953 and 1973, real median family income grew 74% in just 20 years so roughly 3% per year. A family at the middle of the income distribution could expect to be meaningfully better off than their parents. Eleanor's household was part of that story. Then growth slowed. Between 1973 and 2023, a full 50 years, real median family income grew just 45%. Less growth which was spread over more than twice as long. The line kept rising, but the pace of the promise changed.

Section 02 · 1967–2025

The economy split in two

America became dramatically cheaper for things you buy after the dream — TVs, clothes, consumer goods. But it became far more expensive for the things you need to start the dream: housing, healthcare, childcare, education.

Section 06 · The data behind the headlines

The fertility squeeze

Fertility decline has many causes — cultural, medical, social. But the economic environment shapes when and whether people form families. The data shows a consistent relationship between rising housing costs and falling birth rates.

1.60 U.S. Total Fertility Rate · 2024 Record low · Well below 2.1 replacement level
The Housing-Fertility Nexus
Total Fertility Rate (left axis) vs. Shelter CPI index 1967=100 (right axis) · Shaded bands = housing surge periods

Sources: World Bank/CDC via FRED (SPDYNTFRTINUSA) · BLS via FRED (CUSR0000SAH1) · Couillard (2024), Univ. of Toronto

A note on causation

The correlation between housing costs and fertility decline is strong and consistent — but causation is harder to establish. Couillard (2024) estimates rising housing costs explain approximately 51% of the fertility decline between the 2000s and 2010s using a structural model. Natural-experiment evidence (van Doornik et al., CEPR 2024) finds causal evidence for the housing-fertility link in other countries. This analysis presents the relationship honestly: the data supports a causal role for costs, but fertility is shaped by many forces simultaneously.

Conclusion

The American Dream
didn't disappear.

1953–1973 · Eleanor's world
Broadly shared prosperity
  • Real median family income nearly doubled in 20 years
  • Median home cost ~3.4× annual income
  • Fertility above replacement — families were financially feasible
  • Childcare not a budget line — single earner could support a family
  • Bottom 90% held 70% of all wages
1979–2000 · The structural break
Growth continues,
divergence begins
  • Top 1% wages begin to separate sharply from the rest
  • College tuition rises 14.9× in real terms since 1978
  • Hospital services diverge from general inflation
  • Gini coefficient rises — inequality now measurable in every decade
  • Women enter workforce — childcare becomes a real cost
2000–Present · Tiffany's world
The dream segmented
  • Median home now costs 4–5× income — out of reach for Tiffany
  • Infant childcare median: $9,025/yr — 21% of Tiffany's income
  • Fertility hits record low of 1.60 in 2024
  • 88% of counties exceed the 7% HHS childcare affordability threshold
  • The dream survives — but only above a certain income threshold

Tiffany's story is not about one woman. It is about how a once-broad economic promise became conditional on income, assets, and timing — and what the data shows when you follow the numbers from Eleanor's world to hers.
